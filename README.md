# Tribology Fundamentals

## Introduction to Tribology

Tribology is the science and engineering of interacting surfaces in relative motion. It encompasses the study of friction, wear, and lubrication between contacting surfaces. The term was first introduced in 1966 by Peter Jost in a landmark report for the UK government, highlighting the enormous economic costs associated with friction and wear. Understanding tribology is essential in fields ranging from mechanical engineering and materials science to biomechanics and nanotechnology.

## Adhesion Theory

Adhesion Theory is one of the fundamental explanations for friction between surfaces. Proposed by Bowden and Tabor, it suggests that friction arises from the formation and shearing of adhesive junctions at the points of real contact between asperities of two surfaces. When two surfaces are pressed together, contact occurs only at the tips of surface asperities, creating high local pressures that cause plastic deformation and cold welding. The force required to shear these junctions constitutes the frictional force. Key concepts include:

- **Real area of contact**: Only a small fraction of the apparent area is in actual contact
- **Junction growth**: The contact area increases due to combined normal and tangential loading
- **Shear strength**: The friction force is proportional to the real contact area times the shear strength of the junction

## Stribeck Curve

The Stribeck Curve is a fundamental diagram in tribology that illustrates the relationship between the coefficient of friction and the Sommerfeld number (a dimensionless parameter combining load, speed, and viscosity). It characterizes three distinct lubrication regimes:

1. **Boundary lubrication**: At low speeds or high loads, surfaces are in direct contact with only thin molecular films of lubricant separating them. Friction is high and wear is significant.
2. **Mixed lubrication**: An intermediate regime where both boundary and hydrodynamic effects contribute. As speed increases or load decreases, the fluid film begins to support part of the load, reducing friction.
3. **Hydrodynamic (full-film) lubrication**: At sufficient speed, a complete fluid film separates the surfaces, and friction is determined by the viscous properties of the lubricant. Friction increases linearly with speed in this regime.

The Stribeck Curve is widely used in bearing design and lubricant selection.

## JKR Contact Mechanics

The Johnson-Kendall-Roberts (JKR) theory, developed in 1971, extends classical Hertzian contact mechanics by accounting for adhesion between elastic bodies. The JKR model predicts that adhesive forces increase the contact area beyond the Hertzian prediction and that pull-off is required to separate the surfaces. Key results include:

- **Contact radius**: The JKR contact radius is larger than the Hertzian prediction due to adhesion
- **Pull-off force**: The force required to separate two elastic spheres is (3/2)πRγ, where R is the reduced radius and γ is the work of adhesion
- **Hysteresis**: Loading and unloading curves differ due to the influence of adhesion
